Introduction
EÔ¨Äicient ground operations are a cornerstone of competitive performance for airlines in today‚Äôs aviation
industry. One of the most significant sub-processes within ground operations is the passenger boarding
process, which directly shapes the turnaround time, i.e. the period an aircraft spends on the ground
between the completion of one flight and the departure of the next. Turnaround time is a key operational
performance indicator: airlines that minimize it can improve aircraft utilization, reduce costs, and
increase schedule reliability [1].
The passenger boarding process is suÔ¨Äiciently important that it often lies on the critical path of the
turnaround timeline, meaning it can directly constrain how quickly an aircraft can be made ready for its
next departure. Empirical and modeling studies show that boarding time not only influences departure
punctuality but also has measurable economic consequences: conservative estimates suggest that a one-
minute reduction in turnaround time can save an airline tens of millions of dollars annually, through
improved utilization of aircraft and crew and reduced delay propagation in network schedules [1].
The complexity of boarding operations grows substantially for wide-body aircraft used on long-haul
routes. A wide-body aircraft, typically accommodates several hundred passengers across two aisles,
often with multiple cabin classes. Because of their larger passenger capacity and more complex cabin
arrangements, boarding times for wide-body aircraft are longer than for single-aisle jets, contributing
to extended turnaround durations.
Reducing boarding time on such aircraft therefore offers a potentially high leverage point for airlines
seeking operational eÔ¨Äiciencies. Shorter boarding can lead not just to reduced turnaround durations but
also to enhanced punctuality, lower gate occupancy, decreased idle time on the ground, and improved
passenger experience. These effects are especially relevant in long-haul operations where long dwell
times at the gate amplify the impact of each additional minute spent boarding.
Despite the obvious importance of eÔ¨Äicient boarding, many legacy boarding policies used by airlines are
not optimized for minimizing boarding time. Academic research across analytical and simulation meth-
ods has demonstrated that alternative strategies (e.g., -in; window-middle-aisle; optimized groupings)
can significantly reduce boarding times compared to traditional methods [1].
This study focuses on the conceptual design of an agent-based model to evaluate boarding strategies for
twin-aisle aircraft with two active boarding doors and cabin class distinctions. By simulating passenger
movement, luggage stowage, and compliance behavior with boarding sequences, the model aims to shed
light on which strategies minimize boarding times and how robust they are under realistic stochastic
conditions.

1 Concept of Operation and
Characterization of the Study
This chapter introduces the operational context and research scope of the aircraft boarding problem
considered in this study. It first describes the passenger boarding procedure for the selected twin-aisle
aircraft configuration, including the main operational inputs, process phases, and performance outputs
of interest. It then defines the research goal and research questions that guide the analysis, and concludes
by delimiting the scope of the model and the assumptions adopted to represent the boarding process in
a tractable but realistic way.
1.1. Passenger Boarding Procedure
This section describes the operational concept of the passenger boarding process considered in this
study.
The operation concerns the boarding of a twin-aisle, wide-body aircraft with one or two active boarding
doors. The aircraft cabin is divided into distinct cabin classes (business, premium economy and econ-
omy), and all passengers are assigned specific seats prior to boarding. Each passenger boards with full
knowledge of their seat location.
The boarding process is characterized by three primary inputs:
‚Ä¢ Aircraft layout: the cabin configuration, including number of rows, seat arrangement per row,
aisle structure (two aisles), door locations, and class separation.
‚Ä¢ Passenger manifest: a complete list of passengers, each with an assigned seat and potential
individual characteristics (e.g., luggage presence, speed, etc.).
‚Ä¢ Boarding policy (strategy): a predefined release mechanism that determines the order in which
passengers are invited to board.
These inputs define the operational scenario under which boarding takes place.
1.1.1. Boarding Procedure
The passenger boarding process consists of the following sequential phases:
1. Access to the aircraft: passengers enter the aircraft through one of the two active boarding
doors. The allocation of passengers to doors may depend on the selected boarding strategy or
cabin class configuration.
2. Movement through the aisle: after entering the aircraft, passengers proceed toward their
assigned row using the available circulation paths within the twin-aisle cabin. While passengers
typically start moving along the aisle that appears most direct from their entry door, they may
adapt their route based on locally perceived congestion conditions. In particular, if prolonged
waiting occurs in one aisle, a passenger may choose to divert to the opposite aisle through available
cross-aisle connections, for example by moving around galley areas where lateral passage is possible.
Additionally, in the economy cabin, passengers without carry-on luggage may take advantage of
locally available gaps inside a row: if the middle seats are unoccupied, they may cross through
these empty seats to transition from one aisle to the other, effectively creating a short-cut that
can reduce exposure to aisle queues.
3. Carry-on luggage stowage: upon reaching their assigned row, passengers who carry hand
luggage stow it in the overhead bin compartment above their row. This process takes time and
may temporarily block the aisle, causing upstream congestion. The duration of luggage stowage
and seat access varies stochastically among passengers.

1.2. Characteristics of the Study
This section defines the boundaries and overall characterization of the study. It clarifies which aspects of
the aircraft boarding process are explicitly represented, which elements are excluded from the analysis,
and under which assumptions the problem is examined.
1.2.1. Research Goal and Research Question
The primary research goal of this study is to evaluate and compare different passenger boarding strate-
gies for a twin-aisle aircraft configuration in order to identify which strategy minimizes total boarding
time under a fixed aircraft layout and assigned seating configuration.
The central research question is: which boarding strategy minimizes boarding time for a fixed aircraft
layout and seat assignment?
In addition to total boarding time, the study addresses the following sub-questions:
‚Ä¢ Which boarding strategy is the most robust under stochastic passenger behavior, as measured by
the mean and variance of boarding time over multiple simulation runs?
‚Ä¢ Which passenger characteristics (e.g., walking speed, luggage presence, compliance probability)
most strongly influence boarding performance?
‚Ä¢ How sensitive are the results to deviations from the intended boarding sequence due to imperfect
passenger compliance?
The objective is not merely to identify a single ‚Äúbest‚Äù strategy under deterministic conditions, but to
optimize performance under realistic stochastic dynamics.

Evaluating different boarding strategies is essential because passenger boarding is one of the ground-
operation sub-processes that most directly affects turnaround performance. For a wide-body aircraft,
this relevance is even greater, since the larger number of passengers, the presence of multiple cabin
classes, and the more complex cabin geometry increase the likelihood of congestion, seat interference,
and aisle blockage. As a result, even small improvements in the boarding sequence may translate into
shorter turnaround times, better punctuality, lower gate occupancy, and a more robust operation under
stochastic passenger behavior. In addition, the boarding literature shows that several strategies have
been proposed and tested, but there is no universal consensus on which one performs best across all
configurations. This makes a systematic comparison particularly relevant for the present study, whose
objective is precisely to compare alternative predefined boarding policies under a fixed aircraft layout
and assigned seating environment.
In the present case, this comparison requires more than simply specifying the order in which passengers
are called.
Since the aircraft under study is a twin-aisle Boeing 787-type configuration with three
candidate access doors, namely a front door, a middle door located near the transition between business
and the rest of the cabin, and a rear door, each strategy must be defined through three elements: the
passenger release rule, the set of active doors, and the rule used to assign passengers to those doors.
This is necessary to avoid ambiguity and to ensure that each simulated scenario represents a coherent
operational policy.
For clarity, the following nomenclature is adopted throughout this section:
‚Ä¢ F: front boarding door.
‚Ä¢ M: middle boarding door.
‚Ä¢ R: rear boarding door.

assumptions:
‚Ä¢ all passengers have an assigned seat before boarding;
‚Ä¢ ‚Äúrandom‚Äù refers only to the boarding order, not to seat choice;
‚Ä¢ once a door assignment has been made by the strategy, passengers do not choose a different door

1.3.1. Random boarding with assigned seats through the middle door
This strategy serves as the main baseline scenario. All passengers have assigned seats and all of them
board through the middle door M. Passengers are released in a uniformly random order, without any
sorting by row, seat position, or cabin sub-zone. Once inside the aircraft, each passenger proceeds
towards the assigned seat according to the movement rules of the model.
This strategy is useful because it provides the least structured benchmark and therefore allows the
effect of more organized policies to be measured relative to a simple reference case. It is also fully
consistent with the assumptions of the current model, which considers assigned seating and predefined
entry conditions. In practice, no commercial airline employs completely random boarding with assigned
seats. This is primarily because airlines rely on structured boarding groups to monetize priority access,
reward frequent flyers with early access to limited overhead bin space, and keep traveling parties (such
as families with children) together to prevent passenger anxiety and operational friction at the gate.

M only.

all passengers use door M.

1.3.2. Random boarding with assigned seats through front and rear doors
This strategy is a practical parallel-boarding variant of the previous baseline, adapted for a wide-body
(WB) twin-aisle environment such as the Boeing 787. Passengers still have assigned seats and are still
released in random order, but two access streams are enabled simultaneously through the front and
rear doors. In order to avoid ambiguity and balance the cabin loading, a deterministic assignment rule
is applied: passengers seated in the forward part of the aircraft use door F, while passengers seated in
the aft part of the aircraft use door R. Within each door queue, the calling order remains random.
Crucially, any robust model of this strategy must account for non-compliant passengers. In practice,
a small fraction of individuals will inevitably fail to follow the assignment rule and board through the
‚Äúwrong‚Äù door, whether due to confusion on the apron, inattention, or a deliberate attempt to bypass a
longer queue. In a wide-body twin-aisle configuration, these non-compliant passengers cause significant
localized congestion, as they must navigate against the primary passenger inflow across the length of
the aircraft to reach their assigned zones.
This configuration is particularly relevant for operational contexts where wide-body aircraft are boarded
at remote stands via dual external stairs. A real-world example of this is the Italian leisure carrier Neos,
which frequently employs simultaneous front and rear apron boarding for its Boeing 787-9 fleet at leisure
destinations (e.g., Nosy Be, Madagascar). Figure 1.1 shows the groups used in this strategy.

‚Ä¢ Group 1 (Zone 1): Business Class (Rows 1‚Äì7).
‚Ä¢ Group 2 (Zone 2): Premium Economy (Rows 8‚Äì13).
‚Ä¢ Group 3 (Zone 3): Economy Class Rear (Rows 30‚Äì38).
‚Ä¢ Group 4 (Zone 4): Economy Class Mid (Rows 22‚Äì29).

1.3.5. Outside-in boarding with front and middle doors
This strategy is the wide-body adaptation of the classical outside-in or WilMA (Window-Middle-Aisle)
logic. Because the aircraft under study features a twin-aisle configuration, the standard narrow-body
terminology is insuÔ¨Äicient to define the policy unambiguously.
Therefore, the strategy is explicitly
defined by the passenger‚Äôs distance from the aisle and customized for the specific seat layouts of each
cabin class.
To systematically eliminate seat interferences, the passenger set is structured into four distinct sequential
boarding groups.
It is important to note that while this wide-body adaptation provides a highly
optimized theoretical benchmark, it remains a typical academic-example policy. It is rarely adopted
in its pure form by commercial airlines due to the logistical complexity and customer dissatisfaction
associated with separating traveling parties across multiple boarding groups.
The boarding sequence is explicitly defined as follows:
‚Ä¢ Group 1 (Priority / Zero Depth): Business Class (1-2-1 layout). Since all seats have direct
aisle access, all business passengers are equally distant from the aisle. They board as a single
priority group through door F.
‚Ä¢ Group 2 (Maximum Depth): Passengers in the main cabins seated farthest from any aisle.
This includes the window seats and the absolute middle seat of the central block in both Premium
Economy (2-3-2) and Economy (3-3-3). They board through door M.
‚Ä¢ Group 3 (Intermediate Depth): Economy Class intermediate seats. This includes the middle
seats of the side blocks and the aisle seats of the central block in the 3-3-3 layout. They board
through door M.
‚Ä¢ Group 4 (Minimum Depth / Aisle): The remaining seats closest to the boarding flow. This
includes the two aisle seats in Premium Economy and the aisle seats of the non-central side blocks
in Economy. They board through door M.
Within each of these specific groups, passengers are released in a uniformly random order.
The fundamental rationale of this strategy is to minimize aisle congestion and seat interferences by
ensuring that passengers who must penetrate deepest into a row are seated before those occupying
seats closer to the aisle. This method represents the theoretical wide-body evolution of the WilMA

1.3.6. Modified reverse pyramid with front and middle doors
This strategy combines the logic of outside-in (WilMA) boarding with a longitudinal rear-to-front

blocking simultaneously. In this wide-body adaptation, the passenger set is divided into five distinct
boarding groups. Business passengers board through door F, while the remaining cabins board through
door M in a four-step sequence that diagonally integrates seat depth and longitudinal location.
To implement this strategy on the modeled seat map, the middle-served cabins are partitioned into a
balanced ‚ÄúForward Zone‚Äù (Premium Economy rows 8‚Äì13 and front Economy rows 14‚Äì23) and a ‚ÄúRear
Zone‚Äù (rear Economy rows 24‚Äì38). The boarding sequence is explicitly defined as follows:
‚Ä¢ Group 1 (Priority): Business Class (rows 1‚Äì7, 1-2-1 layout). Since all seats have direct aisle
access, they board as a single priority group through door F.
‚Ä¢ Group 2 (Rear Farthest): Rear-zone passengers seated farthest from any aisle. This includes
the window seats and the absolute middle seat of the central block in the rear Economy section

2 Conceptual Agent-based Model
This chapter presents the conceptual agent-based model of the aircraft boarding process considered in
this study. Its purpose is to identify the relevant agents, the environment in which they operate, their
local properties, and the main forms of interaction that jointly determine the evolution of boarding. The
chapter remains at a conceptual level, without yet introducing the formal state language and temporal
specification used in the following chapter.
2.1. Modelling Perspective
The boarding process is modeled as a decentralized multi-agent system in which the global evolution
of the cabin emerges from the concurrent behavior of many individual passengers. Each passenger acts
autonomously on the basis of local observations, internal decision rules, and personal characteristics such
as walking speed, luggage status, and compliance level. There is no central decision-making entity that
dynamically controls passenger movement after entry into the aircraft. Instead, macroscopic outcomes
such as congestion formation, aisle interference, row blocking, and total boarding time arise from the
repeated interaction of locally acting agents under shared physical constraints.
In this model, elements such as doors, aisles, seats, bins, and galleys constitute parts of the environment
that constrain or enable passenger actions. Similarly, the boarding strategy is not represented as an
intelligent coordinating agent. It is treated as an exogenous policy that determines the release order
and initial entry conditions of passengers before the decentralized boarding dynamics unfold.
This modelling choice is consistent with the objective of the study. The aim is not to represent a human
supervisor actively redirecting flow during boarding, but to evaluate how different predefined release
mechanisms perform when passengers subsequently move and interact in a realistic but locally governed
manner.
2.2. Environment Characteristics
The environment of the boarding agents consists of all non-agent entities, spatial structures, and physical
constraints that passengers can perceive and act upon during the boarding process. It is implemented
as a discrete directed graph representing the cabin layout and the admissible movement infrastructure.
The environment includes:
‚Ä¢ the aircraft cabin topology (twin-aisle geometry, seat rows and seat locations, galleys and struc-
tural partitions, class boundaries);
‚Ä¢ a certain number of active boarding doors, which define entry points and induce localized inflow
constraints;
‚Ä¢ the movement infrastructure encoded in the graph (aisle segments, cross-aisle connections, galleys,
row-access nodes and transitions).
In addition to its fixed spatial structure, the environment contains state-dependent resources that evolve
over time as a direct consequence of agents‚Äô actions. These include the occupancy of aisle nodes, the
availability of row access, and temporary blockages created when passengers stop to stow luggage or
negotiate seat entry.

Environment Classification
Under the modeling assumptions of this project, the environment is deterministic: given the complete
current state and an agent‚Äôs selected action, the resulting state transition is uniquely determined by the
transition rules. There is no stochasticity in the physical effect of actions; randomness, when present,
originates from agent-level behavioral parameters rather than environmental physics.
The environment is physically static but dynamically stateful. The cabin layout, doors, and movement
graph do not change over time. However, the occupancy state of nodes and the accessibility of paths
evolve continuously as agents move and interact.
From the perspective of individual agents, the environment is partially observable. Passengers are
modeled with local perception rather than global knowledge. An agent can observe nearby node oc-
cupancy, immediate movement feasibility, and whether the next row entrance is blocked, but cannot
access complete and perfectly up-to-date information about the full cabin state (e.g., congestion levels
beyond line of sight or future stoppages ahead). Decision-making is therefore based on local information,
and macroscopic boarding patterns emerge from decentralized, locally informed behavior.
2.3. Agent Characteristics
The model contains a single agent type: the passenger. Although all passengers belong to the same
conceptual agent class, they are heterogeneous in their individual properties. Each passenger has a
unique assigned seat, enters through a designated boarding door, and follows a personal boarding
trajectory shaped by local conditions and behavioral parameters.
Passengers are modeled as bounded-rational agents. They are not assumed to compute globally
optimal paths or to know the exact future behavior of the rest of the cabin. Instead, they react to
nearby opportunities and constraints while pursuing a clear objective: reaching and occupying their
assigned seat. Their decisions are therefore guided by a combination of local perception, internal state,
simple decision rules, and fixed personal characteristics.

2.4. Dynamic Behaviour of Passenger Agents

2.3.1. Static Characteristics
Each passenger is associated with a set of time-invariant characteristics defined at the start of the
simulation.
These static properties represent passenger heterogeneity and determine how different
individuals behave under otherwise similar conditions.
The most important static characteristics are the assigned seat, the assigned entry door, and the in-
tended boarding position determined by the selected boarding strategy. In addition, passengers may
differ in preferred walking speed, lateral mobility inside the row, compliance level, possession of carry-on
luggage, expected luggage stowage duration.
These properties do not change during boarding, but they influence the evolution of the passenger‚Äôs
dynamic state and therefore indirectly affect overall boarding performance.
2.3.2. Perception
Passenger behavior is based on local perception rather than on full system awareness. At each stage
of boarding, a passenger may observe properties of the nearby environment that are relevant for action
selection.
These observations include whether adjacent or nearby movement locations are occupied or free, whether
forward progress in the current aisle is possible, whether an opposite-direction passenger is present,
whether a row entrance is blocked. Passengers can also detect interaction cues from nearby passengers.
The only interaction that will be explicitly modeled refers to the need to clear a row for another seat
entry.
2.3.3. Internal State
In addition to static properties and external observations, each passenger maintains an internal state
that supports decision-making over time. Conceptually, this internal state includes current location,
progress toward the assigned seat, luggage-handling status, recent movement history, and beliefs about
local movement opportunities or obstacles.
The internal organization of the passenger agent follows a bounded-rational, goal-directed perspective.
A passenger observes the local environment, interprets these observations in terms of its current ob-
jective, and then commits to a next action. In this sense, the model is compatible with a BDI-style
interpretation: passengers maintain beliefs about nearby conditions, pursue the desire to reach and
occupy their assigned seat, and form short-term intentions such as advancing, waiting, switching aisle,
stowing luggage, or entering the row.
2.3.4. Actions
At the conceptual level, the actions available to a passenger correspond to the elementary activities
required during boarding. These include moving to an adjacent position in the environment, waiting in
place, initiating or continuing luggage stowage, sitting down in the assigned seat.
Not every action is available at every moment. The feasible action set depends on the passenger‚Äôs
location, current luggage status, row-access conditions, and the occupancy of nearby cabin positions.
For example, a passenger may be unable to move forward if the next location is occupied.
2.4. Dynamic Behaviour of Passenger Agents
The dynamic behavior of an individual passenger agent can be described as a repeated cycle of per-
ception, evaluation, and action. After entering the aircraft through the assigned door, the passenger
repeatedly observes the local environment, updates its current assessment of the situation, and selects
the next action that best supports progress toward the current goal.
While the assigned row has not yet been reached, the passenger primarily focuses on forward movement
through the aisle system. During this phase, the passenger may advance, wait, or consider switching
aisle if congestion persists and a feasible cross-aisle opportunity exists. Once the assigned row is reached,
the passenger may need to perform luggage stowage before attempting row entry. If row access is blocked
by seated or standing passengers, a local interaction takes place until seat access becomes feasible. The
individual boarding sequence of the passenger ends when the assigned seat is reached and the seating

2.4.1. From Individual Behavior to Global Dynamics
Although the flowchart describes only the local behavior of one passenger, the full boarding process
arises from the simultaneous execution of many such local cycles. The global system dynamics therefore
emerge from the coupling of many decentralized passenger decisions through shared environmental
constraints. Delays, queues, blockages, and changes in aisle usage are not imposed from above, but
arise from the accumulation of local responses to nearby conditions.
This perspective is central to the agent-based modeling approach adopted in this work. Total boarding
time is treated as an emergent system-level outcome generated by many local interactions rather than
as the direct result of a predetermined aggregate flow equation.

2.5. Interactions
This section describes the main forms of interaction represented in the conceptual boarding model.
2.5.1. Passenger-Environment Interactions
A large part of the boarding process consists of interactions between passengers and the environment.
Passengers observe the local state of the cabin and act upon it, while their actions in turn modify that
state. For example, the occupancy of an aisle position influences whether a passenger can move forward;
conversely, when a passenger moves or stops, the local occupancy pattern changes for others. Similarly,
the availability of overhead bin space may trigger luggage stowage, and the act of stowing luggage may
temporarily block the aisle and alter the environment experienced by following passengers.
These interactions are therefore bidirectional.
The environment constrains passenger behavior, but
passenger behavior continuously updates the effective operational state of the environment.
2.5.2. Passenger-Passenger Interactions
Passengers also interact with one another. Direct local interaction occurs in specific situations. One
example is a head-on conflict, in which two passengers moving in opposite directions cannot both
proceed simultaneously through the same local space. Another example is a row-entry interaction, in
which one passenger needs access to a seat while another passenger currently occupies or blocks the
required row space. In such cases, local deconfliction rules determine who waits, who yields, and when
movement can resume.
The model does not assume rich verbal communication among passengers. Instead, interaction is mostly
embodied in movement, waiting, local yielding, and implicit response to the position or needs of others.
This level of abstraction is appropriate for the boarding process considered here, since the relevant
coordination is highly local and short-range.
2.5.3. Interaction Patterns Relevant to Boarding Performance
Two interaction patterns are particularly important for boarding performance.
The first is aisle interference. This includes queue formation, congestion propagation, and occasional
head-on conflicts caused by passengers choosing different local paths in the twin-aisle layout. These
interactions directly affect movement eÔ¨Äiciency and may lead passengers to wait or switch aisle.
The second is row-access interference. This occurs when a passenger has reached the assigned row but
cannot immediately move to the assigned seat because another passenger must first stand, step aside,
or otherwise clear the path.
Together, these two forms of interaction connect local passenger behavior to the main performance out-
comes studied later in the report, including total boarding time, congestion severity, blockage frequency,
and robustness under stochastic passenger behavior.

3.4.1. Predicate Syntax
To express that a particular property holds in a given state of a trace, we use the predicate
holds(state(Œ≥, t), œï)
The expression means that the property œï is true in the state of trace Œ≥ at time t.
In this formalization, temporal reference is carried only by the outer expression holds(state(Œ≥, t), œï). The
predicates that appear inside œï are themselves not indexed by time. For example, predicates such as
believes(p, luggageStatus(s)), observes(p, free(n)), intends(p, advance), and acts(p, moveTo(n)) de-
scribe properties of a state, while the time at which they hold is specified externally through the
trace-state notation.
For example, the statement
holds(state(Œ≥, t), observes(p, position(n)))
indicates that, in trace Œ≥ at time t, passenger p observes that its current position is node n.
Similarly,
holds(state(Œ≥, t), acts(p, moveTo(n)))
indicates that, in trace Œ≥ at time t, passenger p performs the action of moving to node n.
For probabilistic action selection, we use expressions of the form
prob(acts(p, Œ±), œÄ)
to denote that, under the local conditions specified by the corresponding property, passenger p performs
action Œ± with probability œÄ ‚àà[0, 1].
In a realized trace, probabilistic action specifications determine which ordinary action predicate acts(p, Œ±)
becomes true in the current state. Thus, probability expressions specify the distribution over the actions
that may be realized, while the trace itself records the action that is actually taken.

Properties related to row deconfliction maneuver
Row deconfliction is modeled as a local interaction that starts when a passenger reaches the aisle-access
node of the assigned row and is ready to enter the row, but cannot yet proceed because one or more
occupied seat nodes lie on the lateral path toward the assigned seat. Readiness to enter the row means
that the passenger either has no carry-on luggage or has already completed luggage stowage. This
ordering ensures that luggage handling is completed before row-entry conflict resolution is activated. In
that situation, the arriving passenger activates the belief that the row is blocked and temporarily waits
at the aisle-access position. At the same time, the passengers currently occupying intermediate seat
nodes on that path become row blockers.
The maneuver is represented in successive local stages. First, the blocking passengers are identified
and the conflict is recognized from both perspectives: the arriving passenger is row blocked, while the
seated passengers detect a row-entry request and recognize themselves as blockers. Second, the blockers
shift outward toward the aisle in order to clear the path: inner blockers move one position closer to
the aisle, while the outermost blocker may temporarily occupy the aisle-side position or, if necessary,
the adjacent aisle-access node. Third, once this outward displacement has been completed, the blocked
passenger performs a single squeeze maneuver with the temporary aisle-side occupant. This squeeze is
formally represented as a reciprocal action that exchanges the positions of the two involved passengers.
After this exchange, the blocked passenger can continue the entry process, while the displaced blockers
can later return to their original seats through the ordinary movement and seating rules.

passenger p, on the side from which p enters the row. Let

Properties related to head-on deconfliction maneuver
Head-on deconfliction is modeled as a local conflict-resolution maneuver between two passengers ap-
proaching each other in the same aisle with opposite intended directions of travel. The interaction
begins when a passenger detects a head-on conflict with another passenger occupying the adjacent aisle
node. The model then determines which passenger has priority. Priority is assigned through a local
right-of-way rule based on luggage status and, when needed, on the relative direction of movement with
respect to the middle of the cabin.
Once priority has been determined, the two passengers follow different local behaviors. The passen-
ger with priority keeps the intended direction of motion and advances as soon as a feasible progress-
improving aisle node becomes free; otherwise that passenger temporarily waits. The passenger without
priority must yield by leaving the aisle and moving into the first available adjacent seat node. This
temporary refuge clears the aisle and allows the priority passenger to pass. While the priority passenger
is still passing, the yielding passenger maintains the refuge position and waits. When the opponent has
moved beyond the conflict location, the corresponding belief is deactivated and the yielding passenger
returns to the normal decision cycle. In this way, the maneuver is represented as a temporary local
clearance of the aisle rather than as a global replanning process.
